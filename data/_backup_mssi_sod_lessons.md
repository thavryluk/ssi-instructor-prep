## mssi-sod-002 — Physics & Chemistry — Pressure

**Q:** What is absolute pressure?
-   **a)** The sum of atmospheric pressure and cylinder pressure
-   **b)** Hydrostatic pressure minus atmospheric pressure
-   **c)** Atmospheric pressure minus hydrostatic pressure
- ✓ **d)** The sum of atmospheric pressure and hydrostatic pressure

**Explanation:** Absolute pressure = atmospheric + hydrostatic. At the surface: 1 bar (atmospheric only, hydrostatic = 0). At 10 m: 2 bar (1 atm + 1 bar from the water column). At 20 m: 3 bar. This is the pressure value used in gas-law calculations (Boyle, Dalton, Henry). (a) cylinder pressure is unrelated. (b) and (c) are differences, not sums — would yield negative or contradictory numbers.

**Explanation:** SAC's three precise qualifiers: RATE PER MINUTE (not per hour), AT REST (baseline, not under exertion), ON THE SURFACE (1 ata, normalised). That standardisation is why SAC is comparable between divers and dives — anyone at any depth and workload can be back-calculated to this baseline (mssi-sod-030 formula). (a) is too vague, missing the rate / rest / surface qualifiers. (b) hour is wrong unit. (d) under-water + effort produces a depth-and-workload dependent number, not a baseline.

## mssi-sod-036 — Decompression Theory — Safety Stops
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** What is the purpose of performing a safety stop during ascent?
- ✓ **a)** To regulate off-gassing by making predetermined stop at a progressively shallower depth
-   **b)** To develop a dissolved nitrogen tension higher than the surrounding pressure
-   **c)** To maintain an ascent rate of 9 meters per minute
-   **d)** To prevent the formation of bubbles as the gases separate out of solution

**Explanation:** Safety stop = a predetermined pause (typically 3 min at 5 m) during ascent that REGULATES the off-gassing rate. By holding at a shallower depth before surfacing, the diver lets the largest gradient pressure-drop happen with extra time, allowing dissolved nitrogen to leave tissues smoothly through the lungs rather than coming out of solution as bubbles. (d) prevents-bubble-formation is the functional OUTCOME of regulated off-gassing, but (a) is the SoD's precise functional wording. (b) describes supersaturation (the state that drives off-gassing), not the stop's purpose. (c) ascent rate is a separate rule.

## mssi-sod-037 — Decompression Theory — Gas Loading
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** What name do divers give the process of gas moving from a high-concentration area to a low-concentration area at the start of a dive?
-   **a)** Supersaturation
-   **b)** Microbubbles
-   **c)** Off-gassing
- ✓ **d)** On-gassing

**Explanation:** Decompression direction lexicon: ON-GASSING happens during descent / time at depth — ambient pressure is high, the gradient drives N₂ INTO tissues until equilibrium. OFF-GASSING is the reverse (ascent / surface interval) — N₂ leaves tissues. SUPERSATURATION is the STATE during ascent where tissue tension > ambient pressure (the driver of off-gassing). MICROBUBBLES are the silent inert-gas bubbles that always form during ascent — usually harmless, but if they grow / coalesce they become symptomatic DCS.

## mssi-sod-038 — Decompression Theory — Gas Solubility
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** Increased pressure on the body makes gases:
-   **a)** Less soluble
- ✓ **b)** More soluble
-   **c)** Has no effect on solubility
-   **d)** Supersaturated

**Explanation:** Direct application of Henry's Law (mssi-sod-019): dissolved gas amount ∝ partial pressure. Higher pressure → more gas dissolves into the liquid (blood + tissues). This IS the mechanism for on-gassing during descent — every metre deeper raises N₂ partial pressure, increases solubility, drives more nitrogen into tissues. (a) is the opposite of Henry's. (c) contradicts the entire framework of decompression theory. (d) supersaturation is a state during ascent, not a direct effect of pressure on solubility.

## mssi-sod-039 — Decompression Theory — Tissue Compartments
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** What does it mean if a body tissue is poorly-perfused?
-   **a)** It has a good blood supply
-   **b)** It eliminates gas quickly
- ✓ **c)** It eliminates gas more slowly
-   **d)** It absorbs gas quickly

**Explanation:** Perfusion = blood flow rate through a tissue → controls how fast gases exchange. WELL-perfused tissues (heart, brain, lungs) = rapid on-gassing AND off-gassing. POORLY-perfused (fat, joints, ligaments, dense bone) = slow gas exchange in BOTH directions. The question asks about elimination, so 'more slowly' (c) is correct. (a), (b) and (d) all describe well-perfused tissue. Practical relevance: slow tissues are the limiting factor for fly-after-diving wait times — even after fast tissues have cleared, slow ones still have residual N₂.

## mssi-sod-040 — Decompression Theory — DCS Mechanisms
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** How do microbubbles increase the risks of decompression sickness?
- ✓ **a)** All answers are correct
-   **b)** They can grow or join together to form a mass
-   **c)** They can grow inside body tissues and cause nerve pain and damage
-   **d)** They can grow inside the cardiovascular system and block blood flow to organs

**Explanation:** Three pathways from harmless silent microbubbles to symptomatic DCS: (b) growth + coalescence forms bubbles big enough to cause mass effects; (c) tissue-bound bubbles compress nerves → pain (Type I 'bends') and can damage spinal cord (Type II); (d) intravascular bubbles can block circulation → tissue ischemia, CAGE, organ damage. All three are real mechanisms — controlled ascent + safety stops keep microbubbles small enough to be harmless.

## mssi-sod-041 — Decompression Theory — Gas Loading
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** A diver becomes supersaturated with inert gas when:
- ✓ **a)** All answers are correct
-   **b)** There is a desaturation gradient
-   **c)** The amount of gas in their blood exceeds equilibrium levels
-   **d)** They ascend

**Explanation:** Three views of the same supersaturation state: (b) the DESATURATION gradient (tissue tension > ambient pressure) IS the supersaturation — it drives off-gassing flow outward; (c) tissue gas tension exceeding the equilibrium level (= ambient PP) is the textbook definition; (d) ASCENT is what triggers it — pressure drops below tissue tension. All three describe the same physical situation from different angles.

## mssi-sod-042 — Decompression Theory — DCS Mechanisms
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.1 Review

**Q:** What are microbubbles?
-   **a)** Small gas bubbles that form only in well-perfused tissues
-   **b)** Tiny inert gas bubbles that dissolve in a diver's bloodstream and tissues
- ✓ **c)** Tiny inert gas bubbles that form in a diver's bloodstream and tissues
-   **d)** Small gas bubbles that a diver breathes out through their regulator

**Explanation:** Microbubbles = tiny INERT-GAS bubbles that FORM (the opposite of dissolve) in the bloodstream and tissues during ascent — silent, normally harmless if controlled by proper ascent rate + safety stop. (a) is too narrow — they form in any tissue with some blood supply, not only well-perfused. (b) inverts dissolve / form — when bubbles dissolve back, the threat is gone. (d) those are exhalation bubbles from the regulator, completely different — visible in the water column.

## mssi-sod-043 — Decompression Theory — History
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** What condition did early caisson workers experience that led to the identification of decompression sickness?
- ✓ **a)** Joint pain and breathlessness
-   **b)** Feeling hungry
-   **c)** Hypothermia
-   **d)** Vomiting

**Explanation:** 'Caisson disease' = the 19th-century original name for DCS. Workers in pressurised caissons (bridge piers, underwater tunnels) experienced joint pain — they hunched over to relieve it, hence 'the bends' — plus respiratory symptoms ('the chokes'). Paul Bert (1878) identified nitrogen as the cause, J.S. Haldane (1908) developed staged decompression — the foundation of all modern dive tables and computers. (b), (c) and (d) are unrelated symptoms.

## mssi-sod-044 — Decompression Theory — History
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** The Haldane model calculates decompression stops in increments of:
-   **a)** 1 meter
- ✓ **b)** 3 meters
-   **c)** 10 meters
-   **d)** 5 meters

**Explanation:** Haldane (1908) used 10-foot increments — ~3 metres in metric. His algorithm allowed a maximum 2:1 supersaturation ratio (ascent halving the absolute pressure), and stops were placed every ~3 m to control off-gassing. Modern recreational tables and most Tec stops still use 3 m increments today, inherited directly from Haldane's framework. (c) 10 m would be a single Haldane stop spanning multiple compartments — too coarse. (a, d) don't match historical or modern practice.

## mssi-sod-045 — Decompression Theory — Algorithms
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** What is the main difference between models and algorithms?
-   **a)** Models and algorithms are both used to write code
-   **b)** Models and algorithms are the same thing
-   **c)** Models calculate future outcomes, algorithms predict them based on initial conditions
- ✓ **d)** Models predict future outcomes, algorithms calculate them based on initial conditions

**Explanation:** In decompression context: a MODEL is the theoretical framework that predicts how gases behave in tissues (Bühlmann ZH-L16, RGBM, VPM-B, Haldane originals) — what it expects to happen physically. An ALGORITHM is the computational procedure that takes the model + initial conditions (current dive profile, breathing gas, tissue loadings) and calculates concrete outputs: NDL countdown, required stops, ascent ceiling. (c) inverts predict / calculate. (a, b) collapse the distinction.

## mssi-sod-046 — Decompression Theory — Algorithms
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** The M-value is the _____ value of nitrogen.
-   **a)** Minimum tolerated supersaturation
-   **b)** Minimum tolerated saturation
-   **c)** Maximum tolerated saturation
- ✓ **d)** Maximum tolerated supersaturation

**Explanation:** M-value (from German Maximalwert) = the maximum nitrogen supersaturation a tissue compartment can tolerate without symptomatic bubble formation. Bühlmann ZH-L16 specifies 16 M-values per compartment, each a linear function of ambient pressure: M = M0 + ΔM·P. Crossing M-value means the compartment's tension exceeds safe supersaturation — DCS risk rises sharply. (a, b) 'minimum' inverts the concept. (c) 'saturation' (without 'super') means equilibrium, which is always tolerated — no decompression rule needed for that state.

## mssi-sod-047 — Decompression Theory — Altitude / Flying
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** Which of the following statements is true about flying after diving and altitude diving?
-   **a)** The diver is exposed to increased atmospheric pressure
-   **b)** The diver's tissues saturate and desaturate
-   **c)** All answers are correct
- ✓ **d)** A reduction in ambient pressure can cause nitrogen to come out of solution

**Explanation:** Both scenarios share the same physics: REDUCED ambient pressure vs sea-level surface. Flying = cabin altitude ~2400 m → ~0.75 ata; altitude lake diving = high elevation surface pressure already < 1 ata. For a diver carrying residual nitrogen, dropping to lower ambient pressure is effectively continuing the ascent → more off-gassing → bubble formation risk. That's why fly-after-diving wait times exist (typically 18-24 h depending on dive profile). (a) inverts the direction. (b) is vague. (c) all-correct sweeps in the wrong (a).

## mssi-sod-048 — Decompression Theory — Algorithms
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** Which type of model tracks bubbles as they grow or shrink during a dive?
- ✓ **a)** Bubble model
-   **b)** Dissolved gas model
-   **c)** SAC model
-   **d)** Haldanian model

**Explanation:** Two major decompression model families: DISSOLVED GAS models (Bühlmann ZH-L16, Haldane originals) track only the dissolved-gas tension in tissue compartments and check it against M-values (mssi-sod-046). BUBBLE models (VPM-B = Varying Permeability Model, RGBM = Reduced Gradient Bubble Model) track the bubbles themselves — their nucleation, growth and shrinkage through the dive — and tend to recommend deeper stops earlier in ascent. (c) SAC is a breathing rate, not a deco model. (d) Haldanian is a subset of the dissolved-gas family, so it doesn't track bubbles directly.

## mssi-sod-049 — Decompression Theory — Safety Stops
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** Why is it important to maintain proper stop depth control during a dive?
-   **a)** To prevent oxygen toxicity during ascent
-   **b)** To increase the time required to reduce nitrogen in the tissues
- ✓ **c)** To maximize the pressure gradient available for off-gassing and reduce the risk of decompression sickness
-   **d)** To allow the tissues to efficiently absorb nitrogen

**Explanation:** Stop depth = optimised balance: shallow enough for a strong off-gassing gradient (tissue tension >> ambient = fast nitrogen elimination), but not so shallow that supersaturation crosses the M-value (mssi-sod-046) and bubbles grow. Example at 5 m safety stop: ambient ~1.5 ata, fast tissues sit well above this → robust off-gassing flow. Going deeper (e.g., 8 m) reduces gradient → slower elimination. Going too shallow (e.g., 2 m or surfacing early) risks crossing M-value → DCS. (a) oxygen toxicity is a PPO₂ issue, not stop-depth; (b) and (d) invert direction.

## mssi-sod-050 — Decompression Theory — Decompression Stops
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** What must a diver do if their computer calculates that inert gas in a tissue has exceeded its M-value?
-   **a)** They must breathe oxygen immediately upon surfacing
-   **b)** They must make an extra long surface interval
- ✓ **c)** They must make staged decompression stops
-   **d)** They must end the dive and surface immediately

**Explanation:** Crossing M-value means the dive is no longer no-decompression — the diver must perform MANDATORY staged decompression stops at progressively shallower depths (e.g., 9 m, 6 m, 3 m) so each compartment off-gasses below its M-value before continuing the ascent. (a) surface O₂ is first-aid for suspected DCS, not the normal procedure to avoid it. (b) surface intervals sit between dives, not within an ascent. (d) 'surface immediately' is the WORST choice — surfacing while supersaturated above M-value almost guarantees DCS.

## mssi-sod-051 — Decompression Theory — Tissue Compartments
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** Haldane divided the body into compartments based on:
-   **a)** How quickly the tissues metabolize oxygen
-   **b)** How large the tissues in that area of the body are
- ✓ **c)** How fast the tissue absorbs and releases gas, and how much pressure the tissue can handle
-   **d)** Where in the body the tissues are

**Explanation:** Haldane's compartment framework is a MATHEMATICAL abstraction — not anatomical. Each compartment is characterised by two numbers: (1) HALF-TIME = rate of gas uptake/release (Bühlmann ZH-L16 spans 4 to 635 minutes across 16 compartments — fast tissues like blood at one end, slow tissues like dense bone / fat at the other); (2) M-VALUE = max tolerated supersaturation, the pressure-tolerance ceiling (mssi-sod-046). (a) oxygen metabolism isn't directly tracked — compartments model inert-gas (N₂, He) loading. (b, d) inverts the abstraction — Haldane explicitly does NOT use anatomical size or location.

## mssi-sod-052 — Decompression Theory — Algorithms
**Source:** mySSI: SoD Lesson 2.2 Review

**Q:** What does a dissolved gas model calculate?
-   **a)** The amount of dissolved gas in the diver's brain at various depths and times during the dive
-   **b)** The amount of dissolved gas in each bubble at various depths and times during the dive
-   **c)** The tracking of one or more bubbles as they grow or shrink during the dive
- ✓ **d)** The amount of dissolved gas in each compartment at various depths and times during the dive

**Explanation:** Dissolved-gas models (Bühlmann ZH-L16, Haldane) track inert-gas TENSION in each abstract compartment across the dive's depth-vs-time profile, then compare each compartment's tension to its M-value (mssi-sod-046, 051). (a) 'brain' is a single anatomical area — Haldane is mathematical, not anatomical (mssi-sod-051). (b) bubbles aren't 'dissolved' — bubbles imply gas has come out of solution. (c) describes the bubble-model family (VPM-B / RGBM, mssi-sod-048), not dissolved-gas.
